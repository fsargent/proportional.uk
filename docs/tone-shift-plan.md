# Proportional.uk Tone Shift Plan

## Goal

Shift `proportional.uk` away from sounding like a thoughtful policy memo defending a thesis, and toward sounding like a clear, playful, trustworthy guide that helps readers _discover_ the problem and _want_ the answer.

The model is **not** “copy Nicky Case’s voice.”
The model is to borrow the underlying strengths of that style:

- begin with a vivid problem, not an abstract framework
- build curiosity before explanation
- use examples before taxonomy
- introduce technical language only after the reader has earned it
- sound light, human, and a little mischievous without becoming flippant
- make the reader feel like they are _figuring something out_, not being handed doctrine

## Core Diagnosis

Right now the branch is stronger politically than the older version, but it still risks feeling like:

- an argument that knows its conclusion too early
- a reform brief written into a website
- a defense of approval voting before the reader even knows what approval voting is
- a site that sometimes explains _the debate around the thing_ before showing _the thing itself_

That creates a cold start problem.

Readers should first feel:

1. **there is a real glitch here**
2. **that glitch comes from the ballot itself**
3. **there may be a surprisingly simple fix**
4. **now I’m ready to compare systems and tradeoffs**

## Tone Principles

### 1. Problem first, framework second

Never open a section with criteria, meta-framing, or coalition positioning if the reader has not yet emotionally understood the bug.

Bad sequence:

- here are evaluation criteria
- here is where the UK debate stands
- here is my framework
- here is the problem

Better sequence:

- here is the weird thing that keeps happening
- here is why it happens
- here is the simple idea that changes it
- now let’s compare the options seriously

### 2. Curiosity over defensiveness

Avoid language that sounds like the site is pre-arguing with critics.

Examples to avoid:

- “This argument is not just...”
- “The real question is not whether...”
- “This site is not dismissing...”

These can sometimes be useful later, but not early.

Prefer language that invites discovery:

- “Here’s the odd part.”
- “The glitch is simple.”
- “The ballot is doing less than people think.”
- “Once you see that, the rest gets easier.”

### 3. Example before abstraction

Whenever possible:

- show a bug
- show a concrete voter dilemma
- show a practical election distortion
- then derive the principle

The principle should feel earned.

### 4. Technical, but not bureaucratic

The site should remain intelligent and serious. But it should not sound like:

- a consultation response
- a think-tank PDF
- a systems-engineering memo with HTML

Replace bureaucratic phrasing with more human, muscular prose.

### 5. Lightness without silliness

Aim for:

- warmth
- clarity
- occasional surprise
- occasional charm
- occasional sharp line

Avoid:

- forced whimsy
- “hello fellow kids” energy
- jokes that make the subject feel unserious

The right feel is:
**smart friend explaining a deep systems bug with delight**
not
**comedian doing constitutional reform bits**

## Structural Plan

## Phase 1: Reorder the argument

### Current problem

The site still sometimes front-loads framing and comparison before the reader has fully internalised the bug.

### Target sequence

#### Section 1 — The glitch

Purpose:

- make the reader feel the pain and weirdness of FPTP
- show that the issue is not just “unfairness” in an abstract sense
- show that the ballot itself creates bad incentives

Desired feeling:

- “Oh. Right. That _is_ weird.”

#### Section 2 — Why this keeps happening

Purpose:

- explain that one-mark ballots compress too much information
- connect tactical voting, spoilers, safe seats, and lesser-evil logic into one conceptual bug

Desired feeling:

- “This is one family of problems, not random failures.”

#### Section 3 — The simple idea

Purpose:

- introduce approval voting in the cleanest possible way
- no heavy advocacy yet
- just the intuitive concept:
  - what if voters could support everyone they’d genuinely be happy with?

Desired feeling:

- “That’s surprisingly simple.”

#### Section 4 — Now compare serious options

Purpose:

- bring in STV, AMS/MMP, open-list PR, AMS+, proportional approval
- comparison becomes easier because the reader now understands what the ballot question is

Desired feeling:

- “OK, now I have a lens to compare these properly.”

#### Section 5 — Implementation and UK reform politics

Purpose:

- situate the ideas in the UK context
- familiar systems, coalition politics, near-term vs long-term paths

Desired feeling:

- “This is not just elegant, it could fit reality.”

## Phase 2: Rewrite section roles

### A. Hero / opening

The homepage opening should not sound like a manifesto or executive summary.

It should do three things:

1. name the problem plainly
2. make it feel concrete
3. suggest that the problem may be more fixable than people think

#### Tone target

Not:

- “This site explores...”
- “This argument examines...”

More like:

- “Westminster elections keep producing a familiar mess.”
- “Too many voters are forced to choose strategically, not honestly.”
- “That’s not just bad politics. It’s a ballot design problem.”

### B. Criteria section

Keep it, but make it feel like a lens the reader has now earned.

It should come after enough setup that the reader thinks:

- yes, those are sensible tests

Not:

- “here is a framework before you know why you need one”

### C. UK PR bridge

Keep it, but reduce the “meta” quality.

The job of this section is not to say:

- “we are not dismissing the existing debate”

Its job is to say:

- “the UK already knows several serious reform families”
- “that’s useful”
- “now here is the extra question this site adds”

### D. Comparison matrix

Good feature, but it should not feel like a detour into policy-admin mode.

Its framing text should be simpler and less defensive.

The matrix should answer:

- what kind of system is this?
- what does the voter do?
- what’s the main tradeoff?
- how plausible is it in UK reform politics?

## Language Rules

## Replace defensive phrases

Avoid or minimise:

- “This site is not...”
- “The argument is not...”
- “The real question is not whether...”
- “This does not mean...” (unless genuinely necessary)

Use instead:

- plain positive statements
- vivid causal statements
- invitations to notice something

## Prefer shorter, more concrete sentences

Especially near the top of sections.

Bad:

- “Any serious Westminster reform has to clear a few tests at once...”

Better when opening:

- “A good reform has to do more than one thing well.”
- “It has to be fair. It has to be usable. It has to fit British politics.”

## Use rhetorical momentum sparingly

Short punchy lines are good when they sharpen the point.
Too many and the site starts sounding over-scripted.

## Use headings that name what the reader is about to get

Good headings are:

- concrete
- directional
- lightly intriguing

Bad headings are:

- too abstract
- too meta
- too self-conscious

## Voice Reference

### Target voice

- smart
- calm
- lightly wry
- curious
- concrete
- delighted by systems
- not trying too hard

### Anti-reference voice

Avoid sounding like:

- a campaign consultant
- a white paper
- a Twitter thread turned into a website
- a clever debater preloading rebuttals

Rewrite:

- hero copy
- ProblemSection

Rewrite:

## Pass 3 — Comparison and visualiser tone

Rewrite:

- comparison matrix intro

- visualiser intro and explanatory copy

## Pass 4 — Polish for rhythm

Final pass just for:

- sentence length variation
- removing repeated phrases

## Immediate Specific Changes I Would Make Next

1. Rewrite the homepage hero so it sounds less like a site description and more like an invitation into the bug.
2. Rename `RankingProblems` section to something less combative.
3. Rewrite the first paragraph of `RatingVsRanking` to make approval feel discovered, not asserted.
4. Rework `SystemChoice` card copy so it feels less productised.
5. Tone-polish the implementation headers in AMS+, Proportional Approval, and Single-Winner Approval.
6. Rewrite the comparison matrix intro so it sounds less like a policy-seminar throat clearing.

## Non-goals

- Do not turn the site into a joke.
- Do not make it childish.
- Do not bury the serious UK implementation argument.
- Do not remove the political realism added by the coalition pass.
- Do not imitate Nicky Case so directly that the voice stops feeling like Felix’s project.

## Summary

The next version of `proportional.uk` should feel like this:

- first: **here is the glitch**
- then: **here is why the glitch happens**
- then: **here is the simple idea that changes the game**
- then: **now let’s compare serious options for the UK**

The site should feel less like it is defending a position, and more like it is helping the reader notice something important.

That is the real tonal move.
